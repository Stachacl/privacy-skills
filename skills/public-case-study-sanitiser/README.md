# Public Case Study Sanitiser

Convert internal examples, customer stories, incidents, or project notes into privacy-safe versions suitable for public sharing, blog posts, presentations, or marketing materials.

## When to use this

- You want to publish a case study or customer story and need to check it is safe to share publicly.
- You are preparing a blog post or LinkedIn post based on internal project work and need to generalise sensitive details.
- You have an internal incident or lesson-learned example you want to share in training materials without identifying anyone.

## The checklist

1. Check whether the content includes a customer or client name without confirmed permission to publish.
2. Check whether the content includes specific dates that could help identify an incident or client.
3. Check whether it includes a specific location (office, city, school, hospital) that is unnecessary.
4. Check whether it includes unique identifying details about the organisation or situation.
5. Check whether screenshots show names, data, or internal interfaces.
6. Check whether staff names are included without their consent.
7. Check whether financial figures (revenue, contract value, budget) are included without permission.
8. Check whether security incidents or vulnerabilities are described in enough detail to be harmful.
9. Check whether a combination of details could allow someone to identify the organisation or person.
10. Rewrite with generalisations: "a mid-sized logistics company," "in late 2023," "the lead developer."

## Examples

- A case study names a client and includes the exact contract value and timeline. These are replaced with a sector description and a general timeframe.
- A blog post includes a screenshot of a dashboard with visible customer names in the data. The screenshot is replaced with dummy data.
- A training slide uses a real incident involving a named employee. The name and identifying details are removed.
