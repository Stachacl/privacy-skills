# Privacy Incident First Response

Work through a first-pass response when data may have been accidentally exposed, shared with the wrong person, uploaded to the wrong place, or disclosed without authorisation.

## When to use this

- You have just discovered that personal or confidential data may have been sent to the wrong person.
- You need to quickly assess what happened and what can be done about it.
- You want a structured starting point before deciding whether to escalate to legal, compliance, or management.

## The checklist

1. Establish what happened and when: what was shared, sent, uploaded, or exposed?
2. Check whether the incident is confirmed or still suspected.
3. Identify what type of data was involved: personal information, customer data, employee data, health data, financial data, credentials.
4. Estimate how many people could be affected.
5. Identify where the data went: wrong recipient, public post, third-party service, unauthorised access.
6. Check what can be contained: can the message be recalled, the file deleted, or access revoked?
7. Check whether the exposure is ongoing or has stopped.
8. Create a written record of what happened and what is known so far.
9. Identify who needs to be informed internally: manager, data officer, legal.
10. Note what is still unknown and what the next steps are.

## Examples

- An email containing a customer order list is sent to the wrong distribution list. The email is recalled where possible and the recipients are contacted.
- A staff member uploads a client contract to a free AI summarisation tool. The vendor terms are checked immediately to understand retention and training use.
- A spreadsheet with employee salaries is shared via a public Google Drive link. The link is revoked and affected employees are notified.

## Not legal advice

This is a practical first-response guide, not legal advice, and not formal breach notification advice. Regulatory notification requirements vary by jurisdiction. Seek legal or compliance advice for any serious data breach.
