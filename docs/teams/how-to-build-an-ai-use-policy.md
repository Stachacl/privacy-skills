# How to build an AI use policy

## Short answer

A good AI use policy does not need to be long. It needs to be clear about which tools are approved, what data must not be shared with AI tools, and what to do if something goes wrong. A one-page policy that staff actually read is more useful than a lengthy document that no one follows.

## What to check

1. List the AI tools staff are currently using or requesting — including tools that may not be formally approved yet.
2. Define approved uses: drafting, summarising, coding assistance, research, ideation.
3. Define restricted uses that require approval before proceeding: customer-facing AI outputs, use with sensitive documents, AI in automated decisions.
4. Define prohibited uses: pasting customer data into consumer AI tools, sharing credentials, sharing confidential business strategy or unreleased product information.
5. List specific data types that must not be uploaded or pasted: customer names and contact details, financial records, health information, HR and employee records, legal documents, credentials and API keys.
6. Define the review and approval process for new AI tools.
7. Define the escalation process if data may have been shared incorrectly.
8. Write a short plain-language summary (3–5 bullet points) that staff can remember.
9. Assign someone to maintain the policy and set a review date.

**Useful policy sections:**

- Purpose and scope
- Approved uses
- Restricted uses (require approval)
- Prohibited uses
- What must not be uploaded or pasted
- How to request approval for new tools
- What to do if something goes wrong
- Staff-friendly summary
